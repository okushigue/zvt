Overview of ZVT

The Zeta Vibration Theory (ZVT), proposed by Jefferson M. Okushigue, offers a revolutionary vision for understanding the universe. It posits that the cosmos did not begin with an explosion, but rather emerged from amorphous vibrations in the quantum vacuum, guided by the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function.

ZVT seeks to unify classical and quantum physics, explaining phenomena from the birth of galaxies to the fate of the universe through a "cosmic symphony" of eternal and self-sustaining vibrations. It aims to answer questions left unresolved by the standard Big Bang model, such as the existence of massive galaxies observed by the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) in a young universe, and the nature of gravity, cosmic expansion, black holes, and dark matter.

  • Definition: For $$\text{Re}(s) > 1$$, the zeta function is defined as $$\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s}$$. It is analytically continued to the entire complex plane, except for a simple pole at $$s=1$$.

    $$\pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s) = \pi^{-(1-s)/2}\Gamma((1-s)/2)\zeta(1-s)$$
