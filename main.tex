\section{Mathematical Review of ZVT}
The Zeta Vibration Theory (ZVT) is founded on a mathematical structure that uses the properties of the Riemann zeta function to describe physical phenomena.

\subsection{Fundamentals of the Riemann Zeta Function}
ZVT is based on the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function, $\zeta(s)$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Definition:} For $\text{Re}(s) > 1$, the zeta function is defined as $\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s}$. It is analytically continued to the entire complex plane, except for a simple pole at $s=1$.
    \item \textbf{Non-Trivial Zeros:} The theory focuses on the zeros $s = 1/2 + it_n$, where $t_n$ are the non-trivial zeros. The first zero is $t_1 \approx 14.134725$.
    \item \textbf{Functional Equation:} The fundamental symmetry of the zeta is expressed by the functional equation:
    \[
    \pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s) = \pi^{-(1-s)/2}\Gamma((1-s)/2)\zeta(1-s)
    \]
    where $\Gamma(s)$ is the Gamma function.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Primordial Scalar Field ($\Phi$)}
The field $\Phi$ is the central component of ZVT, whose dynamics are governed by the zeta zeros.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Spectral Interpretation:} ZVT posits that the zeros $t_n$ are eigenvalues of a self-adjoint operator $\hat{H}$, such that $\hat{H}\psi_n = t_n\psi_n$, where $\{\psi_n\}$ forms an orthonormal basis.
    \item \textbf{Field Expansion:} The primordial scalar field $\phi(x,t)$ (or $\Phi(\tau)$ in a simplified representation) is expanded into modes associated with these zeros:
    \[
    \phi(x,t) = \sum_n a_n(t)\psi_n(x)
    \]
    \item \textbf{Mode Dynamics:} The temporal coefficients $a_n(t)$ are governed by the differential equation:
    \[
    \frac{\partial^2 a_n}{\partial t^2} + \left( \frac{t_n^2}{\Lambda^2} \right) a_n = 0
    \]
    \item \textbf{Form of the Field $\Phi(\tau)$:} In some representations, the field is described as:
    \[
    \Phi(\tau) = \sum_n w_n A_0 \exp(-0.5 (\tau \omega_n)^2) \cos(\omega_n \tau)

    where $\omega_n = t_n / \tau_{\text{scale}}$ are the vibrational frequencies and $A_0$ is a base amplitude. The weights $w_n$ are defined as:
    \[
    w_n = \frac{\log(n+2)}{|\zeta(1/2 + i t_n)|^2 (1 + 0.01 t_n^2)}

    \item \textbf{Euclidean Propagator:} The propagator connects the zeros to quantum dynamics and the Planck mass ($M_{\text{Pl}}$):
    \[
    \Phi(\tau) = \frac{8}{\pi} \frac{\Lambda^3}{M_{\text{Pl}}^2} \sum_n \frac{1}{t_n^2} e^{-t_n \tau / \Lambda}
    \]
\end{itemize}

\section{The Riemann Hypothesis Proof in ZVT}
The stability of the universe in ZVT intrinsically depends on the validity of the Riemann Hypothesis. If there existed a non-trivial zero of the zeta function $s_n = \sigma + i t_n$ where $\sigma \neq 1/2$, the behavior of the fundamental field $\Phi(\tau)$ and, consequently, the energy density $\rho(\tau) = |\Phi(\tau)|^2$ would become asymmetrical, complex, and divergent.

Such a scenario would lead to chaotic and unstable energy density, contradicting the observation of a coherent, organized, and stable universe. Therefore, the condition that the real part of all non-trivial zeros is $\text{Re}(s_n) = 1/2$ is a physical requirement for the stability and observable existence of the cosmos in ZVT, effectively "proving" the Riemann Hypothesis within the scope of the theory.
